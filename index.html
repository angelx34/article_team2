New Graduates Unemployment: Impacts of AI
By: Saul


To be completely honest, I too am finding it hard to break into today’s job market as a Computer Science major. I remember spending hours last winter combing through listings, just trying to find that one internship that matched my experience level. Even then, it felt like I was throwing my resume into a void.

According to Handshake, a platform widely used by college students and recent graduates, entry-level corporate roles tailored to this group have declined by 15%, while applications per job have surged by 30%. It’s safe to say that OppNet Fellows, no matter current year, will have to navigate uncertain waters throughout college and beyond. And there’s little reason to believe this trend will reverse any time soon — in fact, it’s likely to accelerate as AI continues to reshape the job market.

Advancements in generative artificial intelligence (generative AI) — tools like ChatGPT, DALL·E, and Midjourney, just to name a few — are rapidly changing how companies operate. Tasks that once required junior employees — such as writing press releases (communications assistants), generating design drafts (junior graphic designers), debugging simple code (entry-level software developers), or compiling research summaries (analyst interns) — can now be handled in seconds by AI. As a result, many of the roles that traditionally gave recent graduates a foothold in the workforce are being reduced, restructured, or eliminated altogether.

For students preparing to enter the workforce, this shift has created a deep sense of uncertainty. Many graduates are finding that the skills they’ve spent years mastering are no longer enough — or no longer in demand. As job descriptions change faster than university syllabi, students are left scrambling to adapt on their own, often without institutional support. The gap between education and employment has never felt wider.

Outside institutions like OppNet and CodePath are actively working to bridge the growing gap between education and the evolving demands of the AI-driven workforce through initiatives like the Next Step Apprenticeship. These organizations offer mentorship, technical training, and career support to students from underrepresented backgrounds, helping them gain the skills and confidence needed to compete in an increasingly automated job market. By creating accessible pathways into tech, they're not only filling knowledge gaps but also empowering a more diverse generation of innovators to shape the future of AI.

This shift isn’t confined to entry-level roles — entire job categories are being downsized or phased out altogether. Software engineering roles, for instance, have declined by 33% since 2020, hitting a five-year low. Another sector seeing major contraction is customer service: AI-powered chatbots and voice assistants have replaced thousands of human support agents, particularly in retail and tech. Microsoft alone saved $500 million by implementing AI in its call centers — while laying off over 15,000 employees in 2025. Even when AI isn’t the direct cause of layoffs, the productivity it enables is fundamentally changing how companies approach staffing.

“I just graduated and now I feel like I’m fighting an uphill battle,” my brother told me. He recently earned a Bachelor of Arts in Computer Science from Cornell University and is now planning to pursue a master’s degree. To help cover the cost, he’s looking for a part-time job. “It’s frustrating knowing the opportunities I expected just aren’t there anymore.”

As AI continues to reshape the job market at an unprecedented pace, it’s imperative that education systems, employers, and policymakers work together to bridge the growing gap between skills and opportunity. Universities must integrate AI literacy and practical training into their curricula, while companies should rethink how they recruit and support early-career talent in this new landscape. Without deliberate action, we risk leaving an entire generation scrambling for footing in a workforce that’s evolving faster than they can keep up. The future of work depends not just on technology, but on how we prepare people to thrive alongside it.

Beyond the Digital Takeover: Why New Graduates Need More Than Just AI Anxiety
By: Lucia


The specter of artificial intelligence stealing jobs has dominated headlines and graduation speeches alike. Yet while new graduates nervously scan job boards wondering if their degrees will be obsolete before they're even hired, we're missing a more nuanced—and actionable—conversation about unemployment in the AI era.

Recent research analyzing 24 high-tech developed countries from 2005-2021 reveals a surprising truth: there is indeed "a negative relationship between AI, machine learning, data science, and unemployment." The feared "displacement effect" is real. But here's what the doomsday narratives miss—the same research shows an equally significant relationship between government spending and unemployment rates. While we obsess over robots taking our jobs, we're ignoring the policy levers that could actually help.

The relationship between AI and employment isn't a simple story of human versus machine. Comparative studies across the United States, Germany, and Japan reveal that the impact of job computerization varies dramatically based on education levels and national approaches to workforce development.

In Germany, job computerization actually increases employment for high-risk occupations—but only when workers have bachelor's degrees or higher. Meanwhile, in Japan, higher computerization correlates with increased employment across all risk groups, again primarily for those with advanced education. The pattern is clear: higher educational attainment serves as a buffer against AI displacement.

But education alone isn't enough. The research shows that "occupations with higher-than-mean educational requirements sustain higher employment levels, regardless of the probability of being fully computerized." This suggests that the solution isn't just about getting more degrees—it's about developing the right skills and ensuring those skills are valued in the market.

Here's where the conversation gets interesting—and where policymakers should pay attention. While businesses and individuals scramble to adapt to AI disruption, Bureau of Labor Statistics data reveals a stark disparity in job availability. There are significantly more job openings in nonfarm and private sectors than in government positions, yet government employment remains one of the most stable sectors for new graduates.

The research on AI's impact consistently shows that government spending has a significant negative correlation with unemployment. This isn't just academic theory—it's a policy tool we're underutilizing. As we worry about AI displacing workers, we could be creating more public sector opportunities that both employ graduates and address societal needs that AI can't fulfill: social services, public administration, and community-focused roles.

The anxiety surrounding AI and employment is understandable, but it's leading to the wrong solutions. Instead of just learning to code or desperately pivoting to "AI-proof" careers, new graduates need a more strategic approach.

First, develop systematic disciplinary thinking. AI excels at understanding complex systems and variable transformations, but it lacks "situational generality"—the ability to apply knowledge flexibly across different contexts. Graduates who can integrate knowledge across disciplines and adapt to changing situations will remain valuable.

Second, embrace the reality that employment is now "an important step in the continuous process of self-development and career growth." The days of stable, predictable career paths are over, AI or no AI. Success requires continuous learning, adaptability, and the ability to work alongside new technologies rather than in competition with them.

Third, recognize that some of the most promising opportunities exist in sectors where human judgment, creativity, and interpersonal skills remain essential. The research suggests that graduates should consider government and public service roles, where job stability is higher and the work often requires the kind of contextual decision-making that AI struggles with.

The AI employment debate has become frustratingly binary: either AI will eliminate all jobs, or it will create new ones to replace those lost. The reality, as the research shows, is far more complex and country-specific. Different labor market institutions, educational systems, and policy approaches lead to dramatically different outcomes.

For new graduates facing this uncertainty, the message isn't to panic or to dismiss AI concerns entirely. Instead, it's to recognize that successful navigation of the AI-influenced job market requires both individual adaptation and systemic support. While graduates work on developing flexible, interdisciplinary skills and maintaining lifelong learning mindsets, policymakers should focus on the proven relationship between government spending and employment.

The future of work won't be determined solely by technological advancement—it will be shaped by the choices we make about education, policy, and how we structure our economies. For new graduates, that's actually good news. It means they have more agency in this transition than the headlines suggest, and it means the solutions involve more than just learning to prompt ChatGPT effectively.

The robot takeover narrative makes for compelling clickbait, but it obscures the real work of building an economy that works for humans and machines alike. New graduates don't need to fear AI—they need better policies, more strategic education, and a job market that values the uniquely human skills they bring to an increasingly automated world.

Degrees Without Skills: How Gen Z’s AI Dependence Is Causing a Job Crisis
By: Aliah


As graduation season rolls around again, thousands of Gen Z college students are entering into the job market with this piece of paper that symbolizes all the “hard work” they've done in the last four years and very little else. Although this piece of paper is considered gold in the working industry it has no value if you can't actually do the work. Too many new grads are entering the workforce with a resume full of AI-written papers but no actual skills. But employers are starting to catch on.

To be honest we know that many are using AI as a source of intelligence rather than a tool to boost skills you already have. Tools like ChatGPT have made it easier than ever to generate robotic essays, solve equations, and even write code with little effort. But that convenience comes at a cost. When students use AI as their only source of intelligence they graduate with credentials but no confidence, no compression of the actual profession they “studied” for and ultimately, no career.

Use this as an example: If a computer science student heavily relies on AI throughout college. They use ChatGPT to complete most of their programming assignments, always pass take home tests with flying colors etc. Four years later, they graduate — only to fail the entrance exam for developer role. Why? Because they don’t know how to code without AI and their degree is just paper they wasted thousands of dollars on.

This isn’t just theory. According to a recent report from the National Association of Colleges and Employers (NACE), only about 53.2% of the Class of 2023 had full-time job offers within six months of graduation which is a huge drop from previous years. Employers emphasized that they are noticing a lack of critical thinking, problem-solving and communication skills as key reasons not accepting many new applicants or failed interviews. These are all areas where over reliance on AI leads to underperformance and an un successful career.

What happens if this trend continues? We could be looking at a workforce crisis where college graduates are underemployed — stuck in low-wage jobs not because they lack education, but because they lack experience and practical ability. With rising tuition and living costs, this is a recipe for financial instability and a frustrated, disillusioned generation.

It’s important to note that the problem isn’t AI itself. Used correctly, AI can be an incredible learning aid. It can help brainstorm ideas, troubleshoot errors, and explain complex concepts. But the line between using and abusing AI is thin — and too many students cross it. The issue is how we’re teaching students to use these tools, and whether colleges are holding them accountable for actual learning.

So what’s the solution?

Colleges need to adapt — fast. That means creating academic policies that encourage responsible AI use while still prioritizing skill development. Professors should integrate AI literacy into their curricula, teaching students when and how to use tools like ChatGPT — and when to rely on their own knowledge. Schools can also introduce more practical exams, in-person projects, and peer-led coding or writing sessions that AI can’t complete for students.

Employers, too, have a role. Companies should consider offering skills-based hiring assessments rather than relying solely on degrees. Apprenticeships and internships — paid and accessible — should be more widely available to help grads gain the hands-on experience they clearly need.

The reality is this: A degree used to guarantee a job. Now, it only opens a door. To walk through it, graduates need something more — real, tested, marketable skills. If we don’t address this now, we risk creating a generation of workers who know how to prompt AI but don’t know how to work.

Let’s not wait until the next wave of college graduates hits the job market unprepared. It’s time to rebuild our education system — with intention, with integrity, and with the future in mind.
